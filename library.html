A library of my favorite Kenyan recipes


Welcome to my library of Kenyan recipes. Being an international student from Kenya, I find myself missing a lot of these dishes as I am not a great cook myself. Therefore, I thought I would share some recipes and pictures on this page in the hopes that I'll be motivated to try and master them. Enjoy!


Snacks/ Pastries

Bhajia



This snack is usually made of thin potato slices, coated in a spicy gram flour and deep-fried. It was a popular street food that I indulged in every day from school when I was younger. Brings back memories each time I have it.

Recipe
Ingredients for the Potato Bhajia
  • 8-10 medium sized potatoes
  • 1 cup of Gram Flour
  • 3 cloves of minced garlic
  • 1 teaspoon of Fresh/powdered ginger
  • ½ teaspoon of turmeric
  • 1 tablespoon of coriander
  • 1 teaspoon of paprika
  • ½ teaspoon of salt
  • Chilli powder
  • Black pepper
  • Chopped coriander
  • 1 cup of water
Ingredients for the dipping sauce
  • 2 large tomatoes
  • 1 large onion
  • 5 cloves of garlic
  • 1 teaspoon of fresh/powder ginger
  • 3-5 fresh chillies
  • Salt to taste
  • Black pepper
Instructions

Peel the potatoes and clean them thoroughly.
Slice then into thin round slices. Not as thin as potato crisps, but not as thick as chips(fries)
Let them soak in clean water for about 5 minutes to help get rid of any extra starch.
Rinse thoroughly about 3 times, until the water runs clear.
Drain the water and dry them completely using either kitchen or paper towels. This step of drying is important because it ensures that the Bhajia batter is not watered down as you coat the potatoes.
Mix all the ingredients of the batter and add the water slowly until you get an even thick consistency.
Aim for a consistency that is slightly thinner than pancake batter.
Dip the dry potato slices into the batter and ensure they are all well coated.
Heat some oil on medium heat until ready to deep-fry.
Drop the potato slices into the oil one-by-one.
Ensure you do not overcrowd the pan because this will bring down the temperature of the oil. Deep fry for 8-10 minutes until they are golden brown.
Remove and place on paper towels to drain the excess oil and repeat until all the potatoes are cooked.
Add all the ingredients into a blender and add 1 cup of water. Blend until everything mixed together.
The consistency does not have to be perfectly smooth.
Alternatively, use a grater for all the ingredients in case you don’t have a blender.
Add the blended mixture into a saucer pan and simmer over low heat for 10 minutes until it thickens and becomes darker in colour.
Remove and serve alongside the hot potato bhajias.

Maandazi



This is a pastry that I enjoyed with tea as breakfast for a huge part of my life. Tastes a lot like a doghnut, looks a lot like beignet but denser.

Recipe
Ingredients
  • 2 eggs, beaten
  • ¼ cup sugar
  • ½ cup milk or milk powder
  • 2 cups white flour
  • 1 tbsp yeast
  • 1 tsp cardamom powder
  • Coconut milk
  • Cooking oil
Instructions

In a small bowl, mix your white flour with your milk powder and sugar and set it aside.
In another bowl, add your coconut milk or fresh milk.
Then add cardamom powder, yeast, and a spoon of cooking oil.
Mix the mixture well and allow it to settle for about 2 minutes.
Add the mixture to the first bowl with flour and knead your dough.
Once the dough feels soft, cover and let it settle for about one hour for it to rise.
Once it has risen, make small balls out of the dough, then put flour on each dough.
Cover it and let it rise again for another 30 minutes.
After 30 minutes, roll out the dough and cut it into different shapes.
Fry the maandazi in hot oil.

Chapati



This is a soft, flaky, chewy flatbread that is a staple part of celebratory meals in Kenya and other East African Countries. I usually enjoy it with curries and different stews as a main meal, and with tea or eggs for breakfast as well.

Recipe
Ingredients
  • 3 cups of wheat flour
  • 1 ⅓ cups of warm water
  • 1 teaspoon of salt
  • 1 teaspoon of sugar
  • Vegetable oil for frying
Instructions

Add 3 cups of flour to a bowl. Add salt, sugar, two tablespoons of oil, and 1 ½ cups of water in a separate bowl.
Stir until the salt and sugar dissolve.
Add the liquid mixture to a bowl containing the wheat flour and mix it thoroughly.
Knead for 15 minutes while adding flour.
Keep doing this until the dough becomes non-sticky.
Add a little oil and continue kneading to make the dough soft.
Cover the dough and let it settle for 1 hour.
After the I hour, divide the dough into 10 - 15 equal parts making ball-like shapes and place them on a flat surface dusted with flour.
Dust flour in the flat place, take one of the balls and start rolling with a rolling pin to a circular shape.
Repeat this process with each of the balls of dough.
Heat a pan and place the rolled out circular doughs on it.
Fry one side of the chapati while you spin it and then flip it.
Keep adding oil while spinning.
Remove the golden brown chapati from the pan and place it on a flat plate.
Repeat the process with every chapati Serve with beef, chicken, beans, or vegetables.

Meals

Pilau



I grew up with this is a festive dish, never missing during special occasions or events. It’s made with rice cooked in a well-seasoned broth of beef or chicken. I can barely make this so I try to every once in a while.

Recipe
Ingredients
  • 500 g beef
  • 800 g of white rice
  • 3 large onions to be chopped
  • ⅓ cup oil
  • 4 cloves of garlic to be grated
  • A small piece of ginger to be grated
  • Chopped coriander
  • Tomato paste
  • Pilau masala
  • 1 tablespoon of cumin powder
  • 1 teaspoon of pepper
  • 4 cinnamon sticks
  • 4 cloves
  • 2 cardamom
Instructions

Chop the meat into pieces, wash, and then boil with some water in a pressure cooker.
After boiling the meat to perfect tenderness, set aside.
In a large pot, heat the oil over medium-high heat.
When the oil is hot, add the chopped onions stirring until golden brown.
Add garlic and ginger paste and stir for about a minute.
Add cumin, cardamom, pepper, cinnamon, and cloves.
Add the boiled meat that you had set aside and stir the mixture.
Cook until golden brown.
Add your tomato paste, stir and then add your washed rice and stir for about two to three minutes.
Add four cups of meat soup or warm tap water.
Add salt to taste, stir, cover and cook on medium heat until it is almost dry.
Reduce the heat to low and cook for 12 to 15 minutes.
When the pilau is done, sprinkle some chopped coriander leaves, let it cool for about 10 minutes, then fluff it up.
Serve it with kachumbari and a glass of blended juice.

Githeri



This is another staple in most schools in Kenya that combines beans and corn. It takes relatively less time to prepare and can be served with a variety of other dishes as well.

Recipe
Ingredients
  • Vegetable oil
  • 1 medium-sized onion to be chopped
  • 1 teaspoon of smoked paprika
  • ¼ teaspoon of chilli ground
  • 1 and ½ cup of beans
  • 1 cup of maize
  • 2 large chopped tomatoes
  • Salt to taste
  • Coriander
Instructions

Boil the maize and beans in one pot until they are soft and edible.
Drain all the water in the pan and set the maize and beans mixture aside.
Heat the oil in a sufuria under medium heat.
Fry the onions in the oil until they are golden brown.
Add your chopped tomatoes and continue stirring.
Add the boiled beans and maize mixture and stir well.
Pour in the paprika while stirring for another minute to prevent burning.
Add 1 cup of water and salt, then cover the sufuria and allow to cook for about 20 minutes.
Sprinkle your chopped coriander leaves, mix well, and serve.

Ugali, Nyama Choma



This is probably one of my best dishes of all time. The Ugali is a form of cornmeal dough made from mostly maize flour and served with grilled goat meat and greens.

Recipe
Ingredients
  • 2 cups white finely ground cornmeal
  • 2 pounds goat meat (ribs or steak)
  • 2 tablespoon cooking oil
  • 1 tbsp salt
  • 2 garlic cloves
  • 1 medium-sized ginger
  • 1 lemon
  • 1 cup warm water
  • 1 tbsp black pepper
  • 4 fresh tomatoes
  • 2 onions
Instructions

Marinate the goat meat

Make small incisions on the meat and place it in a large bowl.
Grind the garlic and ginger, place in the bowl, and add squeezed lemon juice and black pepper.
Toss the meat with the spices, making sure it’s well spread on the meat.
Cover the bowl and leave to marinate for 2 hours (you can refrigerate if you like).
Grill the meat

Light a grill and let the coal get hot before placing the meat over the fire.
Once the grill is hot enough, place the meat on the grate.
Add salt and cooking oil to the cup of water.
Turn the meat periodically to cook on both sides while basting it with the salt water.
Once the meat turns golden brown, slice it to see if it’s cooked inside.
Wrap it in foil when it’s done and place it on low heat or the warming rack while you prepare to serve.
Cook the ugali

Bring the water to a boil in a medium pot.
Slowly add the cornmeal while stirring with the wooden cooking stick.
Reduce the heat to low and keep turning the ugali as it begins to stick together and have a dough-like consistency.
Keep turning and pressing the ugali until it is stiff and there are no spots of raw cornmeal. It may be a workout, but that’s how you know you’re making ugali! Cover and let it steam for 10 minutes.
You’ll know it’s ready when it has an aroma of roasted corn.
Pull out of the fire and turn over the pot onto a plate. The ugali will fall out.
Use the wooden cooking stick or spatula to shape it into a round ball or disk while still hot.
Cut into the desired number of pieces while serving.
Prepare the kachumbari

Wash the tomatoes.
Dice the onions and tomatoes.
Mix them in a bowl and add a pinch of salt.
Chop the nyama choma into small pieces, or as preferred, and place on a plate. Add a piece of ugali and 2-3 tsp kachumbari.
